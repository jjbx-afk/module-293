The King of Instruments

"The King of Instruments" due to its central role in Arabic and Levantine music.

The oud is a pear-shaped, fretless string instrument considered the king of Arabic music. It has a warm, resonant tone that is both haunting and intimate. Its name translates to "wood," a nod to its construction, which features a wooden body and a short neck. Played with a plectrum, the oud's deep and complex sound evokes rich emotions and profound storytelling.

The oud is central to exploring maqamat (melodic modes) in Arabic music. Its fretless design allows seamless glides between notes, enabling intricate ornamentations and microtonal expressions. Common maqamat played on the oud include Maqam Rast (majestic and uplifting) and Maqam Bayati (soft and sentimental).

Feature Details
Instrument Type String
Number of Strings Typically 11 to 13, arranged in pairs
Material Wood (spruce for the face, walnut or maple for the body)
Origin Mesopotamia (Iraq), later developed in the Arab world
Playing Technique Played with a plectrum (risha) to produce deep, resonant tones
Significance A central instrument in Arabic and Levantine music, often called the "King of Instruments"
Notable Use Used in classical Arabic music, maqamat performances, and folk traditions
