Introduction to Levantine Music

The Levant, encompassing countries such as Syria, Lebanon, Jordan, and Palestine, boasts a rich and deeply rooted musical tradition. This music, at the crossroads of Arab, Mediterranean, and Oriental influences, relies on a variety of iconic instruments.

These instruments, often handcrafted by local artisans, are used to perform classical compositions, folk songs, improvisations (taqsim), and even to accompany dances and spiritual rituals. Each instrument embodies a part of the cultural soul of the Levant, evoking emotions ranging from joy to melancholy through their unique sounds.

What Are The "Maqamat"?

A maqam is like a musical "mode" or "scale", but much more than that. It’s a framework of notes and intervals (the spaces between the notes) that forms the foundation of a melody. Each maqam is tied to a specific mood or emotion, almost like a musical color palette.

In Western music, you have major and minor scales. Major feels happy, and minor feels sad. In Arabic music, maqamat expand this idea into dozens of unique "emotional shades."

For example: Maqam Rast feels strong and uplifting, like a confident sunrise. Maqam Hijaz feels mysterious and exotic, like walking through an ancient marketplace. Maqam Bayati feels warm and tender, like a mother singing to her child.

What Makes Maqamat Special?

Microtones: Unlike Western scales, which have 12 fixed notes in an octave (C, C#, D, etc.), maqamat use notes in between these, called microtones. Think of them as the "invisible" notes Western ears aren’t used to hearing. They create unique melodies that feel richer and more expressive.

Ornamentation: Arabic music loves embellishments, slides between notes, rapid trills, and emotional flourishes. These are built into the maqam system, giving it a human, vocal-like quality.

Modulation: A single piece of Arabic music can shift between maqamat, like changing emotional gears. This keeps the listener engaged and adds depth to the performance.

How Is It Used?

Maqamat aren’t just theoretical they are felt in the music. Musicians use them to improvise (taqsim) and compose melodies. The choice of maqam depends on the occasion, the mood of the audience, or the story being told.

Think of maqamat as an ancient language of emotion in music. Even if you don’t understand the technical details, you can feel what the music is saying whether it’s joy, sorrow, mystery, or love.
